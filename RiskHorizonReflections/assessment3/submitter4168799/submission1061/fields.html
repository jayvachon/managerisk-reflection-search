This game reflects the struggle of most people or businesses who have to handle complex and uncertain scenarios, often only equipped with scare resources. Even with some sort of information (e.g. probability of impact), we find ourselves having to second guess to make partially informed decisions. The comet information provided knowledge of the threat, while the land raising helps to create protection from the threats and the insurance feature helps with coping after the comets strike - all while trying to achieve the specific goal of growth.

With limited time before impact, I had to make split second decisions on one prevention measure versus another. i.e. Should we continue with research or raise the land to minimize impact? If the risk levels and extent of possible damage were low, I took risks to continue building instead of increasing protection. When I found the risks great or possible damages high, I put my efforts into improving the protection levels. 

During the game, I found myself having to delay repair decisions as well, in case another impact occurs shortly. There were times when there were double impacts in a short space of time, showing that shocks can often hit us multiple times, even before we recover from the previous one. However, delaying the repairs also slowed down the income rate, which could lead to further cashflow issues further down the game. 

I felt it was better to always retain some buffer money in case of emergency needs. To create this buffer, I had to delay upgrades and this affected my growth rate. A good balance of spend versus save had to be struck in order to fulfil the requirements to continue to the next stage. 

Insurance was a good feature as comets struck in every level. It was better to spend on insurance than the bear the full effects of the losses. This in turn, facilitates the recovery rate as you only need to spend a fraction on the repair costs and get you back on track quicker. In real-life, insurance can help businesses cope with the negative effects of the crisis and create more resilience towards potential threats such as financial downturns. 

Repeating the game, I found myself employing different strategies. I stopped quickly building, and instead, concentrated on surveying the comets and their possible impacts. I delayed repairs (or did partial repairs) in case another nearby comets struck twice. I spent more time on research and increase the ground height then purely going for upgrades. Instead of buying the cheaper insurance options, I found myself buying the best protection I could afford. 

Although there are only three variables in this game, it shows the volatile environment that all of us live in. To make the game more interesting and challenging, we could add in the option of increasing more variables in the higher stages. For example, building wear-and-tear could be added to decrease the efficiency of the revenue generation. This will require maintenance of the buildings to be added as a measure of risk management.
