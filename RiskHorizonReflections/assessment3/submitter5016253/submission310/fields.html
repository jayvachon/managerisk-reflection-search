The role of risk management in the game was to point out the importance of community connections in mitigating the impact of risks. In addition, it points to the need for a good balance between knowledge and insurance in risk management. Finally, the timeliness of making these decisions is equally important. You may have a wonderful concept of how to harmonize knowledge, community connections and insurance but a delay in execution flaws the entire planning. Therefore, how and when the execution of the foregoing elements are very important.

Trade-offs in terms of knowledge, protection, and insurance was pretty hard because I needed to make some crucial decisions in light of scarce resources. I think this could be a possible area some of us could get stuck. Using myself as an example, I had to weigh the pros and cons of my decisions. How many pods should I sacrifice for insurance? I have had a number of comets and assessed their risks, am I not familiar with them? So why should I continue to do more research on them? I almost yielded to the urge to create little pods and invest more in knowledge and insurance. But then, I reconsidered the thought. I realized that would be bowing to the risks and failing to pursue opportunities available (in the form of land space) to me to create more pods. I decided to go by the maxim, “Problems are inevitable but misery is optional”. I built and protected my pods.

I approached this game with the mindset of engineering development in the face of risks. I prioritized opportunities and to actuate that, I was keen on creating more pods, making community connections and insuring them collectively (I decided against insuring each pod because that would be cost ineffective).

I was proactive. I made sure I connected communities and insured them collectively before the onset on risks. I also evaluated the severity of impending comets which formed an objective basis for me to decide whether to consolidate protection or insurance. I did most on most occasions. But as I expanded my pods, I prioritized insurance over community protection.

The game was interesting. I think giving it a human face would greatly enhance it. It would make some of us see more than just a game, put us in the hot shoes of decision making and offer us still a hypothetical but then a nearly real experience.
