1) The role of risk management in the game was to evaluate the danger of incoming comets and attempt to mitigate the impact of comets while still maintaining forward development momentum.  The comets represented negative shocks or 'changes in the world' that varied in strength, likelihood and timing. Research represented greater knowledge allowing for better preparation. Insurance policies represented the transfer of resources from safe times and states to cover need and the protection button represented the expenditure of resources on preparing for dangers. 

Initially, I prioritised keeping my station at a higher level of preparation than the danger threatened by incoming comets. Using this strategy, I did not need insurance to redistribute resources as protection and coping was sufficient for the shock. This strategy generally increased resilience, but development was limited and I was eventually unable to reach the goal. This was a ‘cautious’ approach to risk management and was liable to result in ‘stagnation' or 'loss aversion'. Preparation reduced development immediately, as the resources were diverted. It increased over time, but it meant that time and resources that could have been applied to development had already been expended, even when the level of risk turned out to have been less than first thought.

2) In subsequent attempts, I was less cautious, focusing my attention on better awareness of risks with high likelihood and severity. The drawback was occasional unexpected shocks. I minimised unavoidable shocks by carefully using insurance to mitigate large numbers of high severity, high chance comets to avoid crisis. 

I learnt that focusing on research to judge the most significant, high chance impacts and regularly using protection was the best course to maintain development and was preferable to a combination of high levels of insurance and ‘coping’. The timing and regularity of shocks can result in 'crisis', so reducing uncertainty through knowledge can reduce vulnerability and allow or a wise allocation of limited resources.

3) In terms of changes to the game, I would add a random ‘purchase’ of either insurance, an amount spent on protection or ‘greying out’ an option for a period of time either after multiple comets strikes or during long periods of inactivity. This would represent problems for risk management associated with public opinion driving action and expenditure. For example, in Australia, government refuses to take action on climate change despite research and expert opinion.
