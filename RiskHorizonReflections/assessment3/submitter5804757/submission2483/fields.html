Whilst the game provides a basic understanding of the necessity and important of balancing all the options available on the table, trade-offs for want of a better expression, it portrays a particularly superficial approach to risk management. Having played the game three times (one of which the game was completed only to announce a ‘fatal error’ - perhaps an ironic indication that the role of RM is never complete as shocks are never likely to disappear altogether) it soon became apparent that ‘early development’ is the trump card. Initially, based on a desire to be a holistic and benign illuminator for my minions I attempted to balance development, physical defence enforcement and knowledge. This consisted of spending whenever money was available on everything. Spending your way out of a crisis indeed. Result: level three collapse as the goal was not reached. I concluded that an over cautious risk strategy impedes development and as such overall goals were not achieved due to a lack of revenue generation. Therefore, it is necessary to focus on the protection of growth as the central (but not only) tenant of success in risk management.

 

On the second attempt, finances were primarily spent on development consequently placing research as a secondary expense and insurance as a luxury for the latter stages (ie. When there was actually something to protect). A process unfolded as follows: the greater the level of development the faster the income stream became subsequently making disaster mitigation easier. As such, with greater wealth accumulation came an increased capacity to purchase top-end insurance, max-out research and improve the physical defences. These actions would also be used more discriminatorily, allowing for tailored responses to particular threats. The conclusion this time was that development is key. The earlier the area develops, the easier it is to mitigate against potential risks as finances can be redistributed to areas of need (notwithstanding an early cataclysmic event of course!). Sadly, this does suggest why countries in the ‘Western world’ are better equipped to deal with disasters; as former leaders of industry and former colonial powers these nations had an incredible head start, and as such, are able to invest in mitigation procedures without it being at the expense of societal development.

 

My suggestion for improvement(s) rests with the implementation of centralised application of development and risk management. Although the game highlights the integral importance of centralised actors, it does so in a particularly superficial way. It assumes that governments are the sole driver of both development and risk management. As such, the scenario is entirely dependent on what is assumed to be governmental ability to provide resources and protection for it's populous. But this implicitly assumes the application of will, something that isn't particularly forthcoming in reality. In fact, in my opinion is a fatal assumption of the game. It would be beneficial to introduce external actors both within (citizens) and beyond (banking industry) the centralised authority’s control that have the capacity to either undermine or enhance the risk management and development options.  An example of one such addition would be to introduce financial organisms such as banking institutions that interact with the central authority in a number of ways including providing loans, needing bailouts and recalling loans. In essence, adding further stress and development levers.
