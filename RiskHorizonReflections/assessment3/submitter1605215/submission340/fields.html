1.       This game, a reflection of the course so far, shows risk management in the context of development (spending money to build pods) and allows the player to decide on the purchase of investing in insurance (reducing the price of rebuilding pods AFTER a disaster), protection (against the tsunami caused by the comet) and the power of research (that allows you to create community connections before the comet hits).  There was a constant trade-off between investing in more and more substantial pods (development) and preparing for the on-coming comet (disaster).  

2.       I first played the game without watching the tutorial – and my results were miserable.  I could not win the first level because I did not understand the cause and effect of my actions.  Once I watched the tutorial, I starting watching the colored meter on the left.   The concept that research and knowledge could affect the preparedness of the inhabitants – and prevent damage from incoming comets – was made clearer by this game. 

3.       If I were to design a Risk Management game, I would start with a real-world case study from a significantly-improved disaster location such as the Bangladesh typhoon preparedness (1970 to 2007) or the food insecurity program in Ethiopia (1999 to 2005).  I would allow players to allocate both a federal and local budget across several realistic options such as :  research (such as a predictive system for the expected disaaster), public knowledge (early warning systems and children and adult education),  protection (actions/physical preparations), insurance (to pay for damage after the disaster), and set-aside for disaster recovery.   After each budget allocation, the disaster would occur – incurring some level of damage and cost – and another budget would then be allocated in preparation for the next.  To make the situation even more realistic, I would have multiple players play the game together as teams so that they would have to agree on their allocation of resources before each disaster – requiring them to debate various scenarios in an attempt to predict the outcome.  Teams would be required to state their strategy and rationale for each round of play.  Teams results could be compared to determine who has most effectively handled the risk management resource allocations through a series of 6 to 8 disasters.
