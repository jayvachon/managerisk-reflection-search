Role of risk management

I have to admit the game was very interesting, and challenging. To me, it provided an appropriate (though simplified) theatre to experiment with investments in managing risk versus encouraging growth. It provided an interesting application of the key tools/processes discussed in the report

  • Knowledge: increased research about the comet provided a greater understanding of severity and likelihood of hitting
  • Protection: investing in basic protection helped weather the negative effects of comet strikes, even in the absence of insurance
  • Insurance: provided a significant buffer against the costs of risk, and allowed greater redirection of funds towards growth of pods. On occasion, I chose to take a small amount of damage since I knew I had insurance coverage
  • Coping: The implementation of community connections as a means of resisting the impact of risk was quite interesting, especially as it was directly linked to knowledge/research

I eventually found that having a reasonable amount of insurance or risk protection helped, but putting too much into it severely hampered my ability to invest in building pods in the early part of the game. On the other hand, having some early risk protection (50% insurance) ensured enough funds regeneration to invest in pods, with the occasional break from investing to manage individual risk events.

 

Repeated plays

The biggest change in strategy between my first attempt and later ones were the extent and timing of risk protection. I am, in general, risk averse. As a result, I initially spent heavily on protecting the downside of risks through building protection and buying insurance; the idea being that this would leave me more risk-free to pursue growth. This was clearly not the most balanced use of resources, and was something I changed in later iterations.

 

Refining the game

I would introduce two changes to future versions of this game.

  1. Predictability of impact: In real life, while research does help predict the severity of risks such as natural disasters and prepare the community to cope with it, the actual impact can often be very different. For example, Hurricane Sandy’s direction and scale of impact were different from what US weather monitors predicted (Source: http://www.nhc.noaa.gov/archive/2012/al18/al182012.discus.012.shtml?). I would therefore include a randomized change in the severity of the impact in the game. While this might seem unfair to some gamers, a carefully bound randomization (say, +/- 20% of declared severity at 100% research) will be realistic

  2. Alternate risk: Risk management is often a trade-off between different types of risk. For example, a country might have to allocate spending between spending on healthcare and food distribution. Both are important issues and pose high risk to citizens, but resources are limited. A country might also have to make decisions between fiscally prudent action and social obligations. Introducing an additional risk (say, a potential monetary collapse) that does not affect the pods but affects the amount of money the player has would make it even more challenging
