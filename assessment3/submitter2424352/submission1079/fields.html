On this game I learnt the basics of what risk management is made of. The first step was to gather information, which meant to observe, evaluate and decide what was the best way to proceed. Then, protection became critical by being one step forward from volatile environment. Finally, insurance is something you prefer to have and not using, rather than needing but not having, just like condoms. Insurance would be paying less money over a long period on time, rather than paying a huge amount of money in case something bad happens. On this game, what I normally did was to keep an eye on every meteor to evaluate whether would impact earth or not. This was a recursive action. What I think was the most important balance was to upgrade each house while creating new ones, and increasing the height of the land as well, trying not to spend more money to afford observation to meteors. Insurance was a most, it was the first thing I did every time I overcome a level. I reached level 3 because time bit me very often, I could not figure out the way to keep growing on time, I would say meteors weakened even though I increased the height of the lands and kept creating new homes. If I had the chance to suggest another item on risk management behavior, I would add a sort of a “bank deposit” in which I had the chance to pass some money into this account, increasing over time (lets say a minute) referring to a “opportunity cost”, which at the end I would have more money to repair any damage or build/upgrade more homes. I think another way to evaluate risk management would be the amount of money you would have on your account. I think I was proactive by always purchasing an insurance every time I passed to another level, and continually looked for meteors in order to prevent any dangerous impact.

Another improvement I thought would be to add paths on each home in case a meteor strikes, the same way we buy time by observing meteors but on a long term, and more structure way by deciding where to build and the strength for each path. Also, I would add “people” to live on each house (lets say a family) in order to increase realism to foster better decisions because this way we picture that there is people at risk, which safety is in our hands. This improvement is related to the other one for paths, which recreates the fact of designing high reliable transportation and communication systems so government, ONG and independent civil groups can work together to decrease negative effects.

Nevertheless, the game was very realistic and exposed the basics of risk management by giving us the chance to decide accordingly to what we thought was the best strategy. I normally play this kind of games on the internet in which I have been able to learn how to prioritize medium and long term benefits versus short term and “comfort zone” decisions.

In conclusion, I think risk management is similar to what Steve Jobs stated a few years before, “Stay Hungry. Stay Foolish”, which means always stay open minded to new forms of protection and insurance, and always keeping an eye on any kind of risks, wether they come in disruptive technologies, systemic treats, etc. that may harm your family or your business.
