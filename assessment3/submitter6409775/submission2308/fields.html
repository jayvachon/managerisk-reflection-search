As the site of the settlement was under constant threat of tsunamis resulting from asteroids impacts in the ocean (and potentially terrestrial), the goal was to not only safely establish a sustainable community, but to ensure the community’s resiliency in the face of these constant threats. As a result, the role of risk management was to: a) inform stakeholders about the types, frequency, and levels of severity of the potential events, assess their possible impact on the development, and monitor their behavior; b) assess the costs and benefits of multiple event scenarios; c) take appropriate and cost effective preventative action to protect the settlement from these risks; d) insure the development as per the levels of assessed risks; and e) prepare and implement appropriate ex post coping responses when an event occurred in order to maintain the integrity of the community.

I ran the game four times. The first time I didn’t really understand the dynamics of the game itself, but after the first attempt I began to understand how to balance the actual development process and investment cash flows with the management of risk-threat information, assessment of the required levels of preventative engagement and protective investment (including the levels of insurance required as per evolving threats), ensure a quick and robust response network to cope with the aftermath of an event, and manage the rapid rebuilding in partnership with the private sector (e.g. insurance).

One of the primary trade-offs I noticed was the need to occasionally strategically slow down the pace of development in response to a combination of anticipated incoming threats and resource limitations (e.g. cash flows), such as the need to balance development expenditures and liquid reserves (e.g. savings) so as to ensure there was cash available for rebuilding should negative impacts from an event materialize.

With regard to knowledge, besides monitoring cash flows, the two main components I was concerned about were the potential of an actual asteroid impact and the resultant severity levels. With this information, I was able to manage investment decisions and resource flows.

Regarding protection, in addition to responding appropriately to the incoming data and cash flows, a physical development design strategy emerged based on establishing the first structures furthest from the coastline and then filling in the development toward the coast as the settlement became stronger and more resilient after having weathered a couple of impact events and being able to bounce back with greater profitability.

Additionally, in partnership with continuously improving risk management practices (or perhaps I should say: “as a result of good risk management practices”), the development became much more profitable over time. My strategy evolved to invest in both development and risk management early on. Cash balances, of course, remained low for the first couple of levels, but as a result of those early investments, the long-term over-all resiliency resulted in larger financial returns (which were then reinvested back into the development), as well as greater community stability. Hence, early investments in preparation for risk positively affected development.

As far as changing or adding an element that could better represent the risk management choices that matter, I have two comments. The first is related to the game itself, and that is to somehow bring more attention to role of cash flow management as a risk management tool. The second is related to an element I might recommend that represent risk management choices in the area I live, Washington, DC. I would recommend somehow including an element of ex post risk management related to industry and employment.
