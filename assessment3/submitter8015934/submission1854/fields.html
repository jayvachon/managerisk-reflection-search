The role of risk management in the game was shown in a simplified manner yet at the same time essential to progressing. You could choose one option at a time leading to decisions needing to be made about what was the most important factor; knowledge, protection or insurance.

In my own attempts I found that my tactics changed slightly each time I restarted the game. I only managed to reach level 2 but I tried over a dozen times to progress further than that – something wasn’t working! In order to progress from level 1 to level 2 it became apparent that I needed to build more pods and expand development. At first I only built and developed 2 or 3 pods but tried to increase levels of protection, this however proved to be too costly in terms of the time I had available and consequently it halted development. 

At the same time I was purchasing insurance of the middle or top standard early on in the game in order to reduce the impact that comets caused as well as trying to raise levels of protection whenever I could, either as pods developed or after all comets had passed.  This was a tactic that I used throughout the game but more importantly I found that when the opportunity for research arose I had to take it in order for development to continue.  In review I see that levels of protection were forgone for the sake of research and development.

In terms of moving on to level 3 I think I would need to have higher levels of protection in order to reduce the impact of disasters. My problem though was working out how to achieve this whilst researching and developing. The speed and intensity at which comets came in level 2 was probably the most difficult aspect of the game for me. 

Although simplified, I think the game successfully communicated the idea that all decisions have impacts. It also doesn’t necessarily matter if a decision is good in the short term because if it isn’t carried out in the context of the bigger plan then it will not have long lasting and successful effects.  This is why the preparation stages for risk didn’t automatically have an effect on levels of development but instead paved the way for reducing the impacts of disasters in the future.

If I was to develop a new version of the game I would try to embed different patterns for the comets to strike each time the game restarted. This is because I found that after numerous attempts I was able to predict which comets were alright to leave alone and which needed the most preparation. For obvious reasons this is not what happens in real life when dealing with disasters.  Alternatively I would add an “advice” function which would supply some hints as to how to develop further. This I think would significantly alter how people play the game and better reflect my situation of living in a developed country with access to help from other nations and the ability to study and copy the successful strategies of other countries.   

I have found the Risk Horizon game to be an effective tool in demonstrating how a balance needs to be found between different components in managing risk for development so to have a successful result.
