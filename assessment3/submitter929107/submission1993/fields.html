The game Risk Horizon simulates a real-world task of building a community from scratch, with the scenario/background built in outer space.

At the first attempt, the game seems pretty easy: you build houses (or space stations?) and upgrade them based on the budget you have. They more you build and upgrade, the more likely for you to keep up with the schedule and fulfill the requirement of keeping the meters green. It is almost like a Monopolies game based on the outer space.

Yet that’s not all of it. The most differentiate factors of the game is the risk of being hit by the asteroids. Not every asteroids are gonna necessarily hit the planet, yet those who hit will bring a massive shock wave and destruction to the community we built. To avoid such destruction, we must accomplish two tasks alongside with building the community: observing the asteroids to buy time for making connection, and lifting the overall height of the community to minimize the damage.

Such involvement of the risk factor expanded the decision matrix of the players: we are constantly facing trade-off as we can’t do all these tasks at one time. Although we are tempted to spend all the time and capital at building and upgrading the building, the threat of asteroids damaging our works forces us to keep an eye on those red shooting stars in the sky.After the first few unsuccessful attempts, we start to pay more respect to the asteroids and spend more time on observing them (or paying attention to insurance), which pays off by offering us more time to make connection and minimize the damages. 

Such dilemma is very much the same with the real-life decision making process: we are tempted to spend all our time and energy on expending our assets, and prefer to believe that we won’t be so unlucky to have the “asteroids” hit our houses. However, risk was and always will be a major factor of our lives and ignoring it will only escalated the possible damages. This game shows us (or even trains us?) to pay attention to the risk factor in overall strategy planning, which is essential to the overall success of a family, a community or even a country. 

In the end, if I were to develop a new version of the game, I would expand the gap of the destruction between a small asteroid hit and a major hit. Or even introducing a systematic risk factor called "supernova explosion" or "aliens attack". For in real life, the risk we face have a wide expansion of destruction level. We might face small and avoidable risk like catching a minor disease, or a major and systematic ones like warfare and natural disaster. The systematic risk cannot be avoid through individual efforts and all must take the hit. It would be more realistic to include such unavoidable factors into the game to simulate real life scenarios.
