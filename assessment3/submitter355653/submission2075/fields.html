This game presents the player with a challenge to balance development goals with the need for an integrated risk management plan including protection, preparation, and coping measures.  Besides necessitating a balance of these elements, the game highlights the importance of sequencing and strategic decision-making during implementation. 

I will admit to a slow start in gauging the appropriate balance between knowledge, protection, and insurance.  Knowledge of risks only goes so far in reducing susceptibility to shocks if it is not integrated into risk management plans and subsequently implemented.   In the game, one needed to take the research (i.e., better understanding of the severity of the risk) and then pour more resources into preventative measures depending on the level of severity.  Naturally, the quality of the information improved as the comet drew closer.

When faced with the initial choices of resource allocation between development and risk management, I needed to fight the urge to throw everything behind my development goals and build with abandon.  Initially I threw my efforts in development and research, but grew discouraged with research when the comets wiped out my fledgling settlement.  This sentiment of frustration undoubtedly plagues decision makers facing a post-disaster environment in real life, letting the need for immediate recovery and rehabilitation crowd out and take precedent over long-term risk management planning.    

After several tries, I quickly learned that throwing resources behind protection, without considering development needs, quickly led to a loss in the game.  While I think most developing states don’t follow this route, the game underscores the fact that skewing towards the extremes of the spectrum, without considering complementary elements like insurance, is likely to result in failure (or, in real life, disaster and hindered development).  

Recognizing that this game presents a fairly simple risk management exercise, the coping element could have certainly have been strengthened.  If tasked with creating Risk Horizon v. 2.0, I would add a simple post-comet assessment to better understand how well my risk management plan worked.  For example, if I kept building settlements on a flood plain, a post-comet assessment or analysis could pick up on this risk and subsequently guide me to build further inland.  This ongoing analysis and learning is absolutely key in reducing susceptibility to future shocks.  In addition, the community connections piece proved a bit weak and not quite clear in how it helped with development goals.  Perhaps better suited for this game would be a role for government and states in deploying resources, ensuring plan execution, and providing various services to support.  This would better demonstrate the interaction between external environments and internal conditions.
